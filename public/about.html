About the System

A stockroom system built for how small teams actually work

SIMS started as a class project, but the problem it solves is a real one: small teams tracking physical stock by hand, with no shared view of what's actually on the shelf.

The Problem

Stock knowledge lives in one person's head

  • Counts are kept on paper or in a spreadsheet that only one person updates, so everyone else is guessing.
  • Low stock is discovered when a customer asks for something that's already gone, not before.
  • Working out how much inventory is worth means manually adding up categories by hand.
  • Nothing is backed up — a lost notebook or corrupted file means starting the count over.
Where SIMS Is Today

Phase 1: a shared, accurate stock record

  • Every product, quantity, price and category stored in one place the whole team can log into.
  • Dashboard summary of total products, units in stock and total inventory value, updated the moment stock changes.
  • Low-stock items flagged automatically, based on a threshold the team controls.
  • Category-level reports without exporting or calculating anything by hand.
  • Accounts are password-protected, with each session signed and expiring after 8 hours.
Where We're Headed

The roadmap beyond this class deadline

Phase 1 replaces the notebook. The phases after it remove the typing — first with a scan, then with sensors that report stock changes on their own, then with a system that starts spotting patterns in that data.

Phase 1
Live now

Foundation — digital stock record

Web-based product registry, real-time dashboard, low-stock alerts and category reports, built on the current tech stack (Node.js, Express, SQLite). This is what's running today.

Planned

Barcode & QR scanning

Every product gets a scannable code. Adding stock or recording a sale becomes a scan instead of a manual form, cutting entry errors and the time it takes to log a batch.

Planned

IoT stock sensors

Weight or RFID sensors on shelving report quantity changes directly to SIMS as items are taken or restocked, so the dashboard reflects real stock without anyone opening the app.

Planned

AI-assisted insights

With enough stock history logged, SIMS can start suggesting reorder timing and flagging unusual drops in stock — patterns a manual count would never catch in time.
